Introduction and Weekly Activity

When one attended TimeLeft events last year, one told oneself, this isn’t what one want. Meeting in a group of 6 is just too large – we split into 2 different groups most of the time unless not everyone attended the meeting; or there are some annoying people who keeps dominating the conversations without giving others the time to talk; or 2-3 hours is just too short for everyone “to have their say” in a group of six. Or people are just talking about topics that’re genuinely not interesting to me – like, can you believe me? There was a guy whom started imitating the British people’s accent seated downstairs 30 minutes into the conversation, and for the next 1.5 hours, the lady seated in front of him was trying very hard to make him happy, while me, sitting next to him, had entirely cut off from their conversation, speaking with the guy in front of me instead.

On the other hand, one first attended TimeLeft with the expectations of how Oxford Muse (by Theodore Zeldin) hosted and is still hosting “Conversation Dinners.” One, unfortunately, never had the guts to email them about joining one, but meeting someone one-to-one, having full focus on them, and discuss various deeper topics “that people don’t normally want to discuss about.” And they pair you with someone so different from you you know someone is not kissing your ass! You had someone to rebut you on every turn at the very beginning, perhaps getting more and more into consensus as you learn more and more about them, seeing from their eyes how old things/events are seen from a fresh new perspective — ahh, the eye-opening wander!

Perhaps you might say one-to-one are not safe because “we’re not meeting in groups.” Believe me, first, you don’t want to meet them somewhere private unless you’re risking your life to. Second, if one-to-one had really been so dangerous, Tinder and Bumble would have gone bankrupt a decade ago. No, the integrated “security” is never a promise that the other person on the other end is “safe”; it might soothe your heart, but ultimately, you’re trusting that the integrated “security” measure can promise you they’re totally safe, and that’s not true. You still have to take precautions whenever you go out and meet people at various places.

Given that not everyone like group conversations, including those that may be the center of attention in a conversation themselves, would it not make more sense to create something for one-to-one, or at most, 4 people, which is about the limit of attention?

Hence, CurioMeet. CurioMeet, or rather, CurioMeet V2 (one’d killed the V1 earlier this month) is in its early stages, still figuring out where to go, and learning from the mistakes that one made when designing V1. A lot of decisions still had to be made, a lot of obstacles yet to come, and despite people feeling relief coming out of “Conversation Dinners” that they’re given a chance to speak with someone so deeply that they know them more than their colleagues whom they’d “known” for 20 years, most, if not all of them, have signed up willingly to participate in something drifted so far from expectations that “they” can’t be a good representation of most of the people in our everyday life. We, the layman, had been afraid to speak our hearts most of the time because we don’t know (and our brain cannot predict, no matter how hard we try) how bad would the situation goes if we really speak it out. Will the person on the other side accept me?

It’s one thing to do the exercise, “Oh, if the other person tell me this, I would’ve accepted him/her” and assume that they would do the same, vs to actually be put on the spot and try to convince yourself that the other person would accept you if you tell him/her the stuffs you’d been keeping to yourself for the past decade(s). Your emotions are real, and they can’t be convinced by logic. This means, even if the smiles are bright on the other side of the river, you and your brain cannot accurately predict how bright that smile would be – you may even predict demise – and in the end, you never take your first step and hence you never experience what you’d so long yearned for.

Ok, maybe not you, maybe someone you know, someone close to you – have you seen them struggle? Did you take the time to observe how they struggle? Have you tried to tell them what they could do and how they reject your suggestions? Have you tried listening to them why do they not want to take the first step even if “the worst thing that could happen” is just the other person walking away and not the end of the world? Have you tried listening to yourself, why did you not take the first step if “the worst thing that could happen” is the person in front of you walking away and not the end of the world?

One really had high hopes on CurioMeet; on the other hand, one’s uncertain about its future. Whether it can or cannot succeed depends on whether you guys find value in the service being offered – how badly do you guys want to meet someone new and speak your mind? And at the same time, how willing are you to listen to someone speaking their mind? Relatively speaking, is the service one’s providing solving any of your problems in life?

One don’t know. One seriously don’t know, despite all the talks about “loneliness epidemic” and all that.

But, do the work, bro, do the work. Just do the work.

Enough with introductions, let us get to what one want to create in curiomeet.neocities.org (you may have come from curiomeet.com, which one’d bought and set up redirection to this page – at least redirection will apply until the service is ready).

There was a topic in 59 Seconds (by Richard John Wiseman) called “Motivations”, and in it, he discussed that research found that one of the way to beat procrastination is to tell your friends and families about what you’re doing – when you go public, you want to preserve your public image, so you tend to finish what you’re doing than procrastinate.

At the same time, it mentioned that you need to set a plan, a proper plan to do it. For this, one fould the 1/5/10 Planasy (i.e. what you want to achieve in 1 year, 5 years, and 10 years) by MJ DeMarco more useful. In his latest book, Choose Hard Live Easy, he revealed that the yearly goal can be split into sub-goals not just monthly, but also weekly, each aligning to the 10-year-goals that you want to achieve. And while in his previous book, he fixated it to 1/5/10, this his newest book, he mentioned it don’t have to be 1/5/10, but could be 1/2/3 or 1/3/5. And he mentioned that, in 2015, Mr. Beast was still struggling for exams in high school, and posted a video to be released in 2025 that he wished he’ll have 1M subscribers by then; by the time the video is released, he had 443M subscribers. That’s working step by step, aligning with your goals, until you realize, eh, some of the goals one no longer want to achieve because one have change, my priorities have change; while other goals, one’d arrived at it even before the 10-years deadline, and now one had new things to aim for.

All in all, you need a plan. And one need a plan.

So since my friend(s) won’t have time to listen to me babbling about something that’s “not their business” every week, and my parents are bad bad bad listeners, one’ll instead publish them weekly on what one wish to accomplish the week after and whether one’d accomplished them, how much one’d accomplished them, in as much details as one think one want to reveal while hiding away those that one don’t want to reveal yet.

This is my first time doing this, so how one do it one might change in the future, but let’s just try.
